## What is a Gaussian process?

A Gaussian process is a **distribution over functions**.

Instead of fitting a single deterministic model $f(x)$, a GP defines a probability distribution over possible functions consistent with observed data.

> **Definition.** A Gaussian process is a collection of random variables, any finite subset of which follows a joint Gaussian distribution.

Formally,

$$
f(x) \sim \mathcal{GP}(m(x),\; k(x,x'))
$$

where

- $m(x) = \mathbb{E}[f(x)]$ is the **mean function**
- $k(x,x') = \text{Cov}(f(x), f(x'))$ is the **covariance function (kernel)**

The kernel encodes assumptions about smoothness and correlation in the function. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is built by evaluating the kernel pairwise: $\Sigma_{ij} = k(x_i, x_j)$.

## Multivariate Gaussian intuition

Gaussian processes are built on the multivariate Gaussian distribution.

A random vector $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ follows a multivariate normal distribution

$$
\mathbf{X} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma})
$$

where

- $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the **mean vector** — the expected value of each component
- $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is the **covariance matrix** — symmetric, positive semi-definite, encoding shape and correlations

The covariance matrix entry $\Sigma_{ij}$ encodes

$$
\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = \mathbb{E}\left[(X_i - \mu_i)(X_j - \mu_j)\right]
$$

The diagonal $\Sigma_{ii} = \sigma_i^2$ gives the variance of each component; off-diagonal entries control the correlation between components.

Suppose we evaluate a function at inputs $X = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}$. Then the function values follow

$$
\mathbf{f} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{m},\; K)
$$

where $K_{ij} = k(x_i, x_j)$ is the covariance matrix generated by the kernel.

![Multivariate Gaussian](/notes/gaussian-process/multivariateGaussians.png)

Key observations from the 2D case:

- **Identity covariance** produces a circular, symmetric distribution
- **Unequal variances** produce an axis-aligned ellipse; shifting the mean relocates the whole distribution
- **Positive correlation** tilts the ellipse so that large $X_1$ implies large $X_2$
- **Negative correlation** tilts the ellipse so that large $X_1$ implies small $X_2$

Understanding Gaussian vectors is therefore the first step toward understanding Gaussian processes. The GP extends this to infinitely many dimensions.

## Marginalisation and conditioning

The reason Gaussian distributions are so useful is that they are **closed under two critical operations**: marginalisation and conditioning. Both operations yield Gaussian results.

Partition the joint distribution into two blocks $X$ and $Y$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{X} \\ \mathbf{Y} \end{bmatrix}
\sim
\mathcal{N}
\left(
\begin{bmatrix} \mu_X \\ \mu_Y \end{bmatrix},\;
\begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_{XX} & \Sigma_{XY} \\ \Sigma_{YX} & \Sigma_{YY} \end{bmatrix}
\right)
$$

### Marginalisation

Drop one block entirely. Integrating out $Y$ gives

$$
X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_X,\; \Sigma_{XX})
$$

Geometrically this corresponds to projecting the joint density onto one axis.

### Conditioning

Observe $Y = y$ and update beliefs about $X$:

$$
X \mid Y = y
\;\sim\;
\mathcal{N}\!\left(
\underbrace{\mu_X + \Sigma_{XY}\Sigma_{YY}^{-1}(y - \mu_Y)}_{\mu_{X|Y}},\;
\underbrace{\Sigma_{XX} - \Sigma_{XY}\Sigma_{YY}^{-1}\Sigma_{YX}}_{\Sigma_{X|Y}}
\right)
$$

Geometrically this corresponds to slicing the joint density at a fixed $Y = y$.

An important property: the conditional covariance $\Sigma_{X|Y}$ does **not** depend on the observed value $y$, only on the structure of the joint distribution. Only the conditional mean shifts.

![Gaussian conditioning](/notes/gaussian-process/conditioning.png)

This conditioning operation is exactly what produces the **Gaussian process posterior**.

## From vectors to functions

A Gaussian process can be viewed as the **infinite-dimensional extension of a multivariate Gaussian**.

Suppose we have $N$ points $x_1 < x_2 < \ldots < x_N$ on the real line. Instead of fitting one function, we model the vector of all function values as a single sample from an $N$-dimensional Gaussian:

$$
\mathbf{f} = \begin{bmatrix} f(x_1) \\ f(x_2) \\ \vdots \\ f(x_N) \end{bmatrix}
\sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma})
$$

Each sample from this distribution is a realisation of the function at those $N$ points. If we plot those values versus the $x_i$, we get a curve. Each dimension index $i$ corresponds to an input location $x_i$, so samples from the multivariate Gaussian become function curves.

![Dimensionality to function](/notes/gaussian-process/dim_to_function.png)

The correlation structure of the Gaussian (determined by the kernel) controls the shape of those functions — smooth, wiggly, periodic, linear, and so on.

## The Gaussian process prior

Before seeing any data we specify a **prior over functions**. We typically set

$$
m(x) = 0
$$

so that the prior is defined entirely by the kernel. Sampling from the prior gives us the space of functions our model considers plausible before seeing any data.

![GP prior](/notes/gaussian-process/prior.png)

These curves represent random functions drawn from the prior. They all live within the $\pm 2\sigma$ band defined by the kernel.

## Kernels

The **kernel** $k(x, x')$ determines the covariance between function values at any two inputs. It encodes our **prior beliefs** about the function: should it be smooth? periodic? linear? how quickly does correlation decay with distance?

### Common kernels

| Kernel | Formula | Character |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **RBF / Squared Exponential** | $k(r) = \sigma^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{r^2}{2\ell^2}\right)$ | Infinitely smooth, stationary |
| **Matern 3/2** | $k(r) = \sigma^2\left(1+\frac{\sqrt{3}\,r}{\ell}\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}\,r}{\ell}\right)$ | Once differentiable, rougher |
| **Matern 5/2** | $k(r) = \sigma^2\left(1+\frac{\sqrt{5}\,r}{\ell}+\frac{5r^2}{3\ell^2}\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt{5}\,r}{\ell}\right)$ | Twice differentiable |
| **Periodic** | $k(r) = \sigma^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{2\sin^2(\pi r / p)}{\ell^2}\right)$ | Periodic with period $p$ |
| **Linear** | $k(x,x') = \sigma_b^2 + \sigma^2(x-c)(x'-c)$ | Non-stationary, linear functions |

where $r = |x - x'|$, $\ell$ is the **length scale**, $\sigma^2$ is the **variance**, $p$ is the **period**, and $c$ is an **offset**.

![Kernel zoo](/notes/gaussian-process/kernel_zoo.png)

What to notice:

- **RBF** produces banding around the diagonal in the covariance matrix — nearby points are correlated. Samples are silky smooth.
- **Shorter length scales** produce narrower banding — correlation decays faster, giving wigglier samples.
- **Matern 3/2 and 5/2** are rougher than RBF; Matern 3/2 samples are only once-differentiable.
- **Periodic** produces repeating diagonal structure in the covariance matrix.
- **Linear** is non-stationary; sampled functions pass through (or near) the offset point.

---

## Kernel combinations

Kernels can be combined to construct more complex structures. Two key operations preserve the positive semi-definiteness requirement:

| Operation | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| **Sum** $k = k_1 + k_2$ | Models independent additive components (e.g. trend + periodicity) |
| **Product** $k = k_1 \cdot k_2$ | Modulates one process by another (e.g. growing amplitude) |

For example a linear kernel plus a locally periodic kernel (periodic $\times$ RBF) can capture data with a linear trend and seasonal variation simultaneously.

![Kernel combinations](/notes/gaussian-process/kernel_combinations.png)

These combinations allow Gaussian processes to represent rich functional behaviour. Kernels can also be composed with functions (e.g. $k(\phi(x), \phi(x'))$ for deep kernels).

## The GP posterior — regression

This is the central result. Given training data $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{m}$ where $y_i = f(x_i)$ (noise-free for now), we want to predict function values $\mathbf{f}_*$ at new test inputs $X_*$.

We form the **joint distribution** over training outputs $\mathbf{y}$ and test outputs $\mathbf{f}_*$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{y} \\ \mathbf{f}_* \end{bmatrix}
\sim
\mathcal{N}\!\left(
\mathbf{0},\;
\begin{bmatrix}
K(X,X) & K(X,X_*) \\
K(X_*,X) & K(X_*,X_*)
\end{bmatrix}
\right)
$$

Then we **condition** on the observations $\mathbf{y}$ (using the conditioning formula from earlier) to obtain the **posterior**:

$$
\mathbf{f}_* \mid X_*,\, X,\, \mathbf{y}
\;\sim\;
\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_*,\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_*)
$$

where

$$
\boldsymbol{\mu}_* = K(X_*, X)\, K(X,X)^{-1}\, \mathbf{y}
$$

$$
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_* = K(X_*, X_*) - K(X_*, X)\, K(X,X)^{-1}\, K(X, X_*)
$$

The posterior mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_*$ is our best prediction. The posterior covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_*$ tells us **how confident** we are — uncertainty collapses near training points and grows in unexplored regions.

![GP posterior update](/notes/gaussian-process/posterior_update.png)

This is **exact Bayesian inference**. Every observation anchors the distribution — the confidence band collapses near training points and inflates between them, giving a calibrated picture of where we know the function and where we do not.

## Noisy observations

In practice, observations are corrupted by noise:

$$
y_i = f(x_i) + \epsilon_i, \qquad \epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \psi^2)
$$

We incorporate this by adding $\psi^2 I$ to the training covariance block:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{y} \\ \mathbf{f}_* \end{bmatrix}
\sim
\mathcal{N}\!\left(
\mathbf{0},\;
\begin{bmatrix}
K(X,X) + \psi^2 I & K(X,X_*) \\
K(X_*,X) & K(X_*,X_*)
\end{bmatrix}
\right)
$$

The posterior equations remain identical — just replace $K(X,X)$ with $K(X,X) + \psi^2 I$:

$$
\boldsymbol{\mu}_* = K(X_*, X)\,(K(X,X) + \psi^2 I)^{-1}\, \mathbf{y}
$$

$$
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_* = K(X_*, X_*) - K(X_*, X)\,(K(X,X) + \psi^2 I)^{-1}\, K(X, X_*)
$$

This means:

- The GP no longer interpolates exactly through the training points
- The fit becomes smoother — it **regularises** the prediction
- The posterior uncertainty at training points is non-zero

![Noise levels](/notes/gaussian-process/noise_levels.png)

Low noise approximates interpolation; high noise produces heavy smoothing.

## Hyperparameters and length scale

Kernels have hyperparameters (length scale $\ell$, variance $\sigma^2$, period $p$, noise $\psi^2$) that profoundly affect the posterior. The single most important one is the **length scale**.

The squared exponential kernel has the form

$$
k(x,x') = \sigma^2 \exp\left(-\frac{(x-x')^2}{2\ell^2}\right)
$$

The parameter $\ell$ controls how quickly correlation decays with distance:

- **Small $\ell$** — correlation decays rapidly, allowing rapid variation (risk of overfitting)
- **Large $\ell$** — correlation persists over long distances, enforcing smoother functions (risk of underfitting)

![Length scale](/notes/gaussian-process/length_scale.png)

## Marginal likelihood

How do we choose the right hyperparameters? We maximise the **log marginal likelihood** (also called the evidence):

$$
\log p(\mathbf{y} \mid X, \boldsymbol{\theta})
=
\underbrace{-\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{y}^\top (K_\theta + \psi^2 I)^{-1}\mathbf{y}}_{\text{data fit}}
\underbrace{-\frac{1}{2}\log|K_\theta + \psi^2 I|}_{\text{complexity penalty}}
-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi)
$$

This automatically balances **data fit** (first term) against **model complexity** (second term) — an automatic Occam's razor.

- The data fit term rewards models that explain the observations well
- The complexity penalty penalises models that are overly flexible
- Together they prevent both underfitting and overfitting

![Marginal likelihood](/notes/gaussian-process/marginal_likelihood.png)

## Gaussian processes vs neural networks

One interesting theoretical connection is that **infinitely wide neural networks converge to Gaussian processes**.

However the two models behave differently in practice.

Neural networks

- scale well to massive datasets
- learn hierarchical representations
- require gradient-based training

Gaussian processes

- provide principled uncertainty estimates
- work very well in low-data regimes
- do not require weight optimisation

## Limitations

Despite their elegance, Gaussian processes have several limitations.

### Computational cost

Training requires inversion of the covariance matrix $K^{-1}$ which scales as $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ for $n$ data points.

### Memory cost

The covariance matrix requires $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ storage.

### Kernel design

Choosing a suitable kernel often requires domain knowledge.

### Large datasets

Neural networks tend to outperform Gaussian processes when large datasets are available.

Various approximations exist to mitigate these issues:

- sparse Gaussian processes (inducing point methods)
- variational Gaussian processes
- deep kernel learning — use a neural network to warp the input space before the kernel
- GPyTorch / GPflow — production-grade libraries with GPU support and variational inference
