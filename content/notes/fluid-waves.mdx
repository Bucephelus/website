## Motivation

Surface waves look simple, but they contain many of the key ingredients of fluid dynamics: moving interfaces, boundary conditions, dispersion, approximation, and the interplay between physical intuition and mathematical modelling.

They also matter in practice. Surface-wave models appear in:

- coastal engineering
- ship hydrodynamics
- tsunami prediction
- environmental flows

More broadly, surface waves are a useful entry point into the study of nonlinear dynamics and continuum modelling.

## Waves as a Universal Modelling Paradigm

One reason waves are such an important topic is that they appear across physics.

Waves describe how systems transmit **energy** and **information**, and how they respond to disturbances. The same broad mathematical ideas recur in many settings:

- **sound waves** in compressible media
- **electromagnetic waves** in vacuum
- **quantum waves** in wave mechanics
- **surface gravity waves** on fluids

The physical details differ, but the mathematical structure is often similar: a restoring mechanism, a notion of propagation, and a relationship between wavelength and speed.

## Describing Surface Waves

We consider waves travelling along the free surface of a fluid.

For this idealised model, the fluid is treated as:

- a **continuum**
- **inviscid**
- **incompressible**

with **gravity** as the dominant body force.

The governing momentum equation is Euler's equation:

$$
\rho\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla \mathbf{u}\right)
=
-\nabla p - \rho g \,\hat{\mathbf{z}}
$$

This expresses momentum balance under the action of pressure and gravity.

## Free-Surface Boundary Conditions

The interface is described by

$$
z = \eta(x,y,t),
$$

where $\eta$ is the surface elevation.

Two boundary conditions govern the free surface.

### 1. Kinematic condition

Fluid particles on the surface remain on the surface:

$$
\frac{D\eta}{Dt} = u_z
$$

This ensures that the interface moves consistently with the fluid velocity.

### 2. Dynamic condition

Pressure is continuous across the interface:

$$
P(x,y,\eta(x,y,t)) = P_0
$$

where $P_0$ is the atmospheric pressure.

These conditions are what make free-surface problems interesting: the boundary is not fixed in advance, but must be solved for as part of the dynamics.

---

## Velocity Potential Formulation

If the flow is **irrotational**, then we can write the velocity field as

$$
\mathbf{u} = \nabla \phi
$$

for a scalar velocity potential $\phi$.

Incompressibility then gives

$$
\nabla^2 \phi = 0.
$$

So the bulk fluid motion is governed by Laplace's equation, while the dynamics are encoded through the boundary conditions.

For a fluid of depth $H$, the bottom boundary condition is

$$
\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} = 0
\qquad \text{at } z=-H.
$$

At the free surface $z=\eta$, the kinematic condition becomes

$$
\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} = \frac{D\eta}{Dt},
$$

and Bernoulli's equation gives the dynamic condition

$$
\rho\left(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2\right)_{z=\eta}
+ P_0 + \rho g \eta = f(t).
$$

This is a standard and very powerful reformulation of free-surface flow.

## Linear Wave Theory

To extract the basic wave physics, we linearise about a flat free surface and seek travelling-wave solutions of the form

$$
\phi(x,z,t) = \phi_0(z)e^{ikx-i\omega t},
\qquad
\eta(x,t)=\eta_0 e^{ikx-i\omega t}.
$$

Solving Laplace's equation together with the linearised boundary conditions yields the dispersion relation

$$
\omega^2 = gk\tanh(kH).
$$

This is the key result of linear surface-wave theory.

The phase speed is therefore

$$
c = \frac{\omega}{k},
$$

so unlike light in vacuum or classical sound in a simple medium, the wave speed depends on wavelength.

---

## Deep and Shallow Water Limits

The dispersion relation has two especially important asymptotic limits.

### Deep water: $kH \gg 1$

Since $\tanh(kH)\approx 1$,

$$
\omega^2 \approx gk,
\qquad
c \approx \sqrt{\frac{g}{k}}.
$$

Long waves travel faster than short waves.

### Shallow water: $kH \ll 1$

Since $\tanh(kH)\approx kH$,

$$
\omega^2 \approx gk^2 H,
\qquad
c \approx \sqrt{gH}.
$$

In this regime, the wave speed depends only on depth.

This explains why tsunamis can travel extremely quickly across the open ocean even when their surface expression appears modest offshore.

---

## Dispersion and Wave Speeds

Dispersion means that different wavelengths propagate at different speeds. In deep water,

$$
c \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}},
$$

so long waves outrun short waves. A localised disturbance therefore spreads out over time. This is one of the most important physical ideas in wave theory: the shape of a wave packet is not preserved if its Fourier components travel at different speeds.

In shallow water the situation is different. Since $c \approx \sqrt{gH}$, the phase speed is independent of wavenumber. All Fourier components travel at the same speed, so wave packets propagate without dispersing. This is why the shallow-water equations can support sharp, coherent structures like bores and solitary waves.

The full relation smoothly interpolates between these two regimes. ## A Special Case: the Kelvin Wake

A particularly elegant consequence of surface-wave theory is the **Kelvin wake** (maybe at some point I will include a note on the Kelvin wave, since it has a lot of interesting theory itself, for now, enjoy this note).

When an object moves across deep water, the wake it produces is confined to a fixed angle:

$$
19.28^\circ
$$

independent of the object's size or speed.

This universality arises from:

- the **dispersive** nature of surface gravity waves
- the **superposition** of many wavelengths
- simple **geometric constraints**

It is a striking example of how linear wave theory can explain a visually complex real-world phenomenon. Reference the duck at the start!

## What This Example Teaches

Free-surface waves are a good example of the standard modelling workflow in applied mathematics:

1. start from governing equations
2. identify the relevant boundary conditions
3. simplify where appropriate
4. solve and interpret the result physically

They also raise important modelling questions:

- Why is linearisation justified?
- What physical effects are lost by neglecting viscosity or nonlinearity?
- When does linear theory break down?
- How do these ideas extend to nonlinear or data-driven PDE models?

## Closing Remarks

Surface gravity waves sit at a very nice intersection of physics, mathematics, and modelling.

They are simple enough to analyse, but rich enough to illustrate many ideas that recur throughout continuum mechanics:

- PDE formulation
- boundary-value problems
- linearisation
- dispersion
- asymptotic limits
- physically interpretable modelling

That is part of what makes them such a useful gateway topic in fluid dynamics.
